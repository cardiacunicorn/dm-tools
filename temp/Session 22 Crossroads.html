Impossible Situations

Sometimes, typically out of combat, you’re gonna want to make a skill check that would be impossible. In standard 5th Edition D&D, what I’d do is just set the DC to 35 or 40 or something, and you could roll, and will inevitably fail. One D&D is playing with the idea that a Nat 20 always succeeds, and a Nat 1 always fails.

I guess I’m interested in your thoughts in how you’d like those moments to play out: if one of you wants to do something impossible, what do you think should happen?

  • Should I get you to roll, and inevitably fail in your attempt?
  • Should I tell you, you can’t do that? Which has the benefit of your character not attempting something crazy, but robs you of agency and the entertainment value of a failure.
  • Should I deem a Natural 20 some sort of divine intervention that means it succeeds to some degree?

Exhaustion or Wounded

I’ve spent a bit of time looking at Pathfinder, which is similar to D&D. It’s got a bunch of interesting mechanics and ideas, one of which is that when a player gets knocked out they gain a Wounded condition when they come back up, like how we use Exhaustion. The Wounded condition applies no penalty to you, and it goes away after a Long Rest, but if you get knocked out again, you automatically fail your 1st Death Save. Hard core. I’m happy with our Exhaustion system, but if you guys want to really introduce some peril, I’ll let you decide whether that’s something you want to implement, even on a player by player basis.

Pathfinder Highlights

If I were to start a new campaign, I’d probably lean towards using Pathfinder 2E

  1. The system is built around balance: I do a lot of work to make sure combat is challenging but fair, and keep an eye on who’s builds I need to factor in, and it’s a really inexact science
  1. It gives you more to do with your money: magic items have set prices
  1. There’s no Advantage and Disadvantage, but you do still get situational bonuses
  1. Interestingly, there’s no weird action rules with bonus actions etc. you just get 3 actions per turn, and a reaction. So that could mean on a turn where you don’t move, you can blast off the equivalent of 3 bonus actions. Or attack three times. Some spells require more or less actions to use, or can be varied e.g.
    1. Spend 1 Action on the Heal spell to touch someone and heal them
    1. Or spend 2 Actions to do it at range
    1. Or spend 3 Actions to heal in a 30ft sphere
  1. Instead of D&D’s simple save or fail, which sucks as a spellcaster, they have 4 results: success or failure as usual, but if you beat the DC by 10 or more, you critically succeed which might mean avoiding damage altogether. Or if you fail by 10 or more you critically fail and take double damage. It also applies to martial attacks too.
  1. Not everything has an opportunity attack, so there’s a lot more movement around the battlefield, and tactical things you can do
  1. There’s 22 classes, all with their own special subclasses, abilities and feats, which is cool. But because you choose from a list of features every single level, all of you could play the same class and play in entirely different ways. It means you could opt to skip the shit aspects of your class and just pick the stuff that enhances how you like to play. You could keep things broad and be a jack of all trades, or really specialise in certain things to be a real expert in your play-style.
    1. You could be a wizard that trains in full armor
    1. Or an alchemist who throws bombs but can heals with a crossbow that launches syringes
    1. Or a warlock who summons and rides beasts into battle
    1. Or a barbarian that transforms into a fire-breathing bear when they rage
  1. Even the same creatures have massively different abilities that really give them much more character. D&D martial monsters like the owlbear and a gnoll just feel the same, they run in, they multiattack, and they stay put. In Pathfinder, the owlbear attempts to pin you down and disembowel you with it’s beak, while the gnoll is a skirmisher who runs in attacks, and darts between you, attacking in a frenzy or running away.
  1. It’s got rules and suggestions for awesome things you can do with your skills proficiencies in-between fights:
    1. Medicine you can heal and cure poison and disease without needing to be a healing class
    1. Nature, Arcana or Religion you can just learn an associated spell, even though you’re a rogue or fighter who doesn’t normally cast things
    1. You can use Deception to feint an attack to gain a bonus to hit
  1. There’s rules for your items being damaged over time, which doesn’t sound too thrilling, but it gives you a reason to visit the blacksmith or learn how to repair your own gear
  1. Downside: learning a new system
  1. Downside: once you’ve built a specialised character, you might find yourself doing the same thing over and over again, but that’s a problem with D&D too

The Greatwood

The Ritual Stone

Alien monolith

Ahead, you spy a mossy knoll rising out of the marsh with a curious standing stone at its center. A jagged, eight-foot-tall monolith of black rock, the stone is laced with veins of crystal that shimmer with a strange light.

Roughly 3 feet across, this enchanted menhir glows with a pale radiance under moonlight, shedding dim light in a 5-foot radius. Any character who succeeds on a DC 15 Intelligence (Arcana) check or a DC 18 Intelligence (Religion) check understands that this ritual stone is an ancient monument to some dark, primordial god, and that it serves modern spellcasters by acting as a focus for illicit rituals and dark incantations. (Bol'bara was using the stone to commune with her patron when she was beset by soldiers of Fort Venture.)

Rite of Communion

Any spellcaster who spends a short or long rest attuning to the ritual stone can use an action to cast the commune spell. Once used, this property of the ritual stone can't be used again until the next full moon. Any warlock who uses the ritual stone to commune with their otherworldly patron gains inspiration and acts as if under the effect of the guidance cantrip for 1d2 + 1 days.

Ritual Stone Fragments

Any character who succeeds on a DC 16 Wisdom (Perception) check made to inspect the ritual stone discovers that three naturally loosened fragments of the stone can be easily removed and replaced. A fragment carries a semblance of the stone's magic, allowing the character who carries it to cast the augury spell. Once used, this property of the fragment can't be used again until the next dusk.

If a fragment is taken more than 10 feet from the ritual stone, its magic fades and it ceases to function after 1d3 + 1 days. A character who attempts to chip away a piece of the ritual stone in addition to the three fragments must succeed on a DC 20 Charisma saving throw or take 3 (1d6) psychic damage.
